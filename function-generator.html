Embedded Function Generator

Back to Portfolio

Designed and validated a Teensy 4.1-based function generator with digital control of waveform, frequency, and amplitude, plus a custom 4-layer PCB and enclosure-ready layout.

Overview

Built a digitally controlled function generator around a Teensy 4.1 for a microprocessor system design project. The system used an AD9833 waveform generator, MCP4132 digital potentiometer, MCP4725 DAC, TL074 op amp stages, and an LMC7660 voltage converter to generate and condition output signals. A 4x4 keypad and 20x4 LCD allowed users to change settings directly from the device.

What It Did

  • Generated sine, square, and triangle waveforms
  • Allowed digital control of waveform, frequency, and amplitude
  • Reached measured frequencies from 1 Hz to 250 kHz
  • Reached measured amplitudes from about 600 mV p-p to 5 V p-p
  • Used oscilloscope measurements to verify output behavior across multiple settings

System Architecture

The design used the AD9833 as the waveform source, the MCP4132 digital potentiometer for amplitude control, the MCP4725 DAC for offset generation, and TL074 op amp stages for signal summing and conditioning. The circuit also used an LMC7660 with capacitors to create the negative rail needed by the op amp stage. User input came from a 4x4 membrane keypad connected to Teensy pins 2 through 9, and feedback was shown on a 20x4 LCD.

Design Decisions

We used header pins so the keypad, LCD, and signal modules could be connected more easily during integration and test. I also added screw terminals for external power and oscilloscope access at the final output stage. The PCB used a 4-layer stackup with signal traces on the first layer, a ground plane on the second, and power traces on the third.

Testing and Results

We validated the design with oscilloscope measurements across multiple frequencies, amplitudes, and waveform modes. The circuit produced sine, square, and triangle waves, and demonstrated operation from 1 Hz up to 250 kHz. Measured output examples included 100 Hz, 1 kHz, 100 kHz, and 250 kHz cases, with amplitudes ranging from about 600 mV p-p to 5 V p-p.

Problems and Fixes

The DAC-based offset stage was not fully integrated at presentation time because of a voltage mismatch with the circuit. As a workaround path, I evaluated using a filtered PWM signal from the Teensy as an alternative offset source. I also ran into a PCB manufacturing issue when JLCPCB flagged the board outline because the edge cuts did not match the copper area correctly. After reviewing feedback and resubmitting files, the board moved into production.

Code and Control Logic

The firmware handled keypad input, updated the LCD, switched waveform modes, adjusted frequency through the AD9833, changed amplitude through the MCP4132, and controlled offset through the MCP4725. The code mapped user commands into device settings and updated the display in real time.

What I Learned

This project forced me to work across embedded control, analog signal conditioning, user interface logic, PCB design, and hardware validation. It also showed where design assumptions break during integration, especially around voltage compatibility and board manufacturing constraints.
